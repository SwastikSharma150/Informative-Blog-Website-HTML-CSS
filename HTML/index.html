Introduction

The North American beaver is a semi-aquatic herbivorous animal with short front legs and is generally identified with its blackish brown hair colour from the outside. Castor canadensis is the scientific name of the American beaver. It is also one of the symbols of the national wildlife of Canada. It is also known as North American beaver or the Canadian beaver.

The North American beaver is a native to North America so it can be seen in the United States Canada and Mexico. It has also been introduced in the following countries:

  • Russia
  • Argentina
  • Germany
  • Finland
  • Belgium
  • Luxembourg

Although the beaver is a native to North America, over the time it spread to other continents of the world like Europe and Asia. The beavers can be found in rivers, marshes, streams, ponds, lakes and wetland areas. While North American beavers are roughly 15 million in population across the North American sub-continent, their very similar sub-species known as, Eurasian beavers are distributed country-wise as follows:

Physical Appearance

A North American beaver has long visible incisors that are worn down over the course of of its life by constant chewing. They have digitated front paws and webbed rear feet. Thanks to their huge lungs, beavers can swim up to 10 km/h and go more than half a mile underwater for up to 15 minutes. They also have whiskers, which help them sense objects surrounding their faces and head which is very helpful in confined spaces and in dark water.

The plump bodies of North American beavers exhibit features like having a yellowish brown dark coat and a large, flat, scaly tail. A beaver's tail can be anything between short and broad to long and narrow, and their vertebrae extend almost to the end of the tail. The nearly hairless tail, which is coated in black scales and used as a balancing aid when working on land and as a warning signal when slapped on the water, serves as a rudder for the animal while swimming. Additionally, beavers have fat reserves in their tails and frequently transition between aquatic and terrestrial habitats. American beavers have a length of 3 to 4 feet and a height of 1 to 1.5 feet. The beaver's tail can take many different forms, but it typically has a base thickness of around two inches and a tip thickness of about 0.24 inches.Their agile, tiny front feet are perfectly suited for functioning on land. They have well-developed digging claws.

Reproduction

American beavers are monogamous, mate for life, and reach reproductive maturity at roughly 3 years old for both genders. Kits develop their swimming ability within a week of birth, when they weigh around 1 pound (0.5 kilogrammes), have open eyes, and are totally coated in fur. On land, moms frequently carry kits on their broad tails or even walk upright while carrying them. Kits may lie on their mothers' backs while submerged.


Above is the sound of a beaver mating call from water. American beavers mate for their entire lifetime and both genders remain sexually active. Between late January and early February, they mate, and the females give birth in the following spring.

According to the Natural Science Research Laboratory of Texas Tech University, pregnant female North American beavers typically remain pregnant for a total of 107 days.

Habitat

Beavers are one of the few creatures who modify their habitat; they create mud-caulked dams that are waterproof using sticks that are weaved with reeds, branches, and saplings. Dams create ponds that move slowly, which slows down stream erosion. These ponds supply water and food for many larger animals as well as habitat for a variety of little aquatic species. Beavers establish new habitats by erecting dome-like lodges that rise 6.5 feet or more and can reach widths of 39 feet that can sustain a very diversified biological community.

They are commonly found in or around freshwater ponds, lakes, rivers, marshes and swamps living in their lodges. Living quarters are found on the top of the lodge, which is above the water line, and a lodge may have one or more submerged entrances. These lodges are frequently constructed inland, forming enclaves that can only be reached when underwater.

Beavers spend the most of the day sleeping. However, in areas where ponds completely freeze over in the winter, beavers may hibernate in their lodges or beneath the ice, surviving on their fat stores and the food they have acquired. North American beavers generally live upto 10 to 12 years.

Diet

Beavers are herbivores that consume leaves, woody stems and aquatic plants. Their primary construction materials: poplar, aspen, willow, birch, and maple - are also their favourite foods. They spend the winter within their lodge chamber in colder locations, eating twigs that have been kept on the mucky pond floor as winter food. The stems are kept cool and their nutritional worth is preserved by the water's role as a refrigerator. Beavers eat corn on the cob-style while holding their meal in their front paws.

According to ADW, unlike other mammals, beavers can digest cellulose, which is a major component of their diet.

Status and Conservation

According to the IUCN Red List, American Beavers are classified as Least Concerned. Due to beavers being previously prized for their pelts, castoreum, and flesh, they went nearly extinct via hunting. The beaver's main predator, besides humans, is the wolf while other predators include bears, foxes and coyotes. They are widely distributed as of now, frequent over most of their habitat, and their population is now stable.
